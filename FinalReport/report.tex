\begin{abstract}
Recommender systems are central to modern online platforms, yet choosing the right collaborative filtering strategy for a given dataset remains an open practical question. We implement and compare four collaborative filtering methods for movie recommendation on the MovieLens-Small dataset (100K ratings, 610 users, 9{,}724 movies): biased matrix factorization with SGD, a deep hybrid model with CNN title and genre encoders, truncated-SVD with Ridge/Lasso calibration, and alternating least squares.
All models are evaluated on rating prediction (MAE, RMSE) and top-10 recommendation (Precision, Recall, F1, NDCG) under an identical per-user 80/20 holdout split.
MF-SGD achieves the best rating accuracy (MAE\,=\,0.663, RMSE\,=\,0.867), while SVD-Ridge produces the strongest ranking quality (NDCG@10\,=\,0.108), revealing a notable disconnect between point-wise and ranking evaluation.
We provide detailed training dynamics analysis, feature-block ablation, and latent-factor interpretation to explain the observed differences.
A full-stack web demo (\textit{StreamX}) is also developed to showcase the trained recommender in an interactive setting.
\end{abstract}



\section{Introduction}

Recommendation systems have become indispensable components of online services---from e-commerce product suggestions to movie and music discovery platforms \cite{ricci2011introduction}.
Among the many families of recommendation algorithms, \emph{collaborative filtering} (CF) relies exclusively on observed user--item interactions to infer preferences, making it applicable even when item content metadata is scarce \cite{resnick1994grouplens}.
The most successful CF paradigm to date is \emph{matrix factorization} (MF), which projects users and items into a shared latent space so that inner products approximate observed ratings \cite{koren2009matrix}.

Despite its conceptual simplicity, the design space of MF-based recommenders is vast.
Optimization may proceed via stochastic gradient descent (SGD), alternating least squares (ALS), or singular value decomposition (SVD);
the loss function may be mean squared error, Huber loss, or a ranking-aware surrogate;
and the model may incorporate side information such as item titles, genres, or temporal signals \cite{zhang2019deep}.
How these choices interact in practice---especially on a moderately sized, long-tailed dataset---is rarely benchmarked in a controlled, apples-to-apples fashion within a single codebase.

In this project, we address that gap by implementing four distinct collaborative filtering models from scratch, training all of them on the identical MovieLens-Small dataset split, and evaluating them with the same metrics suite.
Our contributions are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
\item We provide a controlled comparison of four CF models---MF-SGD, a deep hybrid CNN model, SVD with linear calibration (Ridge/Lasso), and ALS---under identical data conditions.
\item We analyze not only aggregate accuracy (MAE, RMSE) and ranking quality (Precision, Recall, F1, NDCG@10) but also training dynamics, latent factor semantics, and feature-block ablation.
\item We build \textit{StreamX}, an end-to-end web application (Django\,+\,Next.js) that serves real-time recommendations from the trained models, demonstrating practical deployment.
\end{itemize}



\section{Problem Definition}

We formalize the recommendation task as follows.
Let $\mathcal{U}$ and $\mathcal{I}$ denote the sets of users and items, respectively, and let $r_{ui}\in[0.5,5.0]$ be the rating that user $u$ assigns to item $i$.
The observed ratings form a sparse matrix $\mathbf{R}\in\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{U}|\times|\mathcal{I}|}$, where only a small fraction of entries are known (density $\approx$ 1.7\%).

\paragraph{Task 1: Rating Prediction.}
Given a training set $\mathcal{D}_\text{train}$ of observed $(u,i,r_{ui})$ triples, learn a function $\hat{r}:\mathcal{U}\times\mathcal{I}\to\mathbb{R}$ that minimizes the prediction error on held-out ratings $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$.
Performance is measured by Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE):
\begin{align}
\text{MAE} &= \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_\text{test}|}\sum_{(u,i)\in\mathcal{D}_\text{test}} |r_{ui}-\hat{r}_{ui}|, \\
\text{RMSE} &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_\text{test}|}\sum_{(u,i)\in\mathcal{D}_\text{test}} (r_{ui}-\hat{r}_{ui})^2}.
\end{align}

\paragraph{Task 2: Top-$N$ Recommendation.}
For each user $u$, produce a ranked list of $N=10$ items that $u$ has not yet interacted with.
The quality of the list is measured against the held-out items in $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$ via Precision@$N$, Recall@$N$, F1@$N$, and NDCG@$N$.
Items are ranked by descending predicted rating.


\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Memory-Based CF.}
Early CF methods compute user--user or item--item similarities directly from the rating matrix and aggregate neighbor ratings for prediction \cite{resnick1994grouplens,sarwar2001item}.
While interpretable, these methods scale poorly and are sensitive to sparsity.

\paragraph{Matrix Factorization.}
Koren et al.\ \cite{koren2009matrix} demonstrated that biased matrix factorization, optimized via SGD, achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on the Netflix Prize dataset.
Hu et al.\ \cite{hu2008collaborative} proposed weighted ALS for implicit feedback, and Zhou et al.\ \cite{zhou2008large} developed large-scale parallel ALS.
Koren \cite{koren2008factorization} further extended MF with neighborhood integration and temporal dynamics.

\paragraph{Deep Recommenders.}
He et al.\ \cite{he2017neural} introduced Neural Collaborative Filtering, replacing the inner product with a multi-layer perceptron.
Cheng et al.\ \cite{cheng2016wide} proposed Wide\,\&\,Deep learning, combining memorization and generalization.
Covington et al.\ \cite{covington2016deep} described YouTube's two-tower deep recommendation architecture.
Wang et al.\ \cite{wang2015collaborative} combined deep autoencoders with CF.
More recently, Zhang et al.\ \cite{zhang2019deep} surveyed the landscape of deep-learning-based recommender systems.

\paragraph{Hybrid Approaches.}
Leveraging item content (titles, genres, descriptions) alongside collaborative signals has been shown to mitigate cold-start and improve generalization.
CNN-based text encoders, following Kim \cite{kim2014convolutional}, are commonly used to extract features from short textual metadata in recommendation contexts.

Our work brings together classical MF (SGD and ALS variants), an SVD-based linear hybrid, and a deep content-aware model within a single comparative framework.



\section{Methodology}

All four models share a common prediction structure:
\begin{equation}
\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu + b_u + b_i + \mathbf{p}_u^\top \mathbf{q}_i,
\label{eq:base}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the global mean rating, $b_u$ and $b_i$ are user and item biases, and $\mathbf{p}_u,\mathbf{q}_i\in\mathbb{R}^k$ are latent factor vectors.
They differ in how the parameters are estimated and whether additional content features are incorporated.

\subsection{MF-SGD}

This model directly optimizes the $L_2$-regularized squared-error objective:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\mathbf{p},\mathbf{q},b} \sum_{(u,i)\in\mathcal{D}_\text{train}} \!\!\bigl(r_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui}\bigr)^2 + \lambda\bigl(\|\mathbf{p}_u\|^2 + \|\mathbf{q}_i\|^2 + b_u^2 + b_i^2\bigr),
\label{eq:mfsgd}
\end{equation}
via element-wise SGD.
At each step, the error $e_{ui}=r_{ui}-\hat{r}_{ui}$ drives gradient updates:
$b_u \leftarrow b_u + \eta(e_{ui}-\lambda b_u)$,
$\mathbf{p}_u \leftarrow \mathbf{p}_u + \eta(e_{ui}\,\mathbf{q}_i - \lambda\,\mathbf{p}_u)$,
and symmetrically for $b_i$ and $\mathbf{q}_i$.

Key design choices: latent dimension $k{=}48$, learning rate $\eta{=}0.005$ with multiplicative decay $0.96{\times}$ per epoch, regularization $\lambda{=}0.04$, a 10\% validation split for early stopping (patience 8), and 50 maximum epochs.
Factors are initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0,0.1)$.
We found that lower regularization than the typical default of 0.05 substantially improves ranking quality at minimal cost to rating accuracy.

\subsection{Deep Hybrid Model}

This model extends the two-tower architecture with item content features.
The \textbf{user tower} maps a user ID through an embedding layer ($k{=}48$) followed by dropout.
The \textbf{item tower} concatenates three feature streams:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Item-ID embedding} ($k{=}48$): captures collaborative signal.
\item \textbf{Title encoder}: tokenized movie titles (max 15 tokens, vocabulary 20{,}000) are embedded ($d{=}32$), processed by three parallel Conv1D layers (kernel sizes 2, 3, 4; 48 filters each), globally max-pooled, concatenated, and projected to $k{=}48$ via a dense layer.
This multi-kernel CNN design follows the architecture of Kim~\cite{kim2014convolutional}.
\item \textbf{Genre encoder}: genre tokens (max 4) are embedded ($d{=}12$) and average-pooled.
\end{enumerate}

The concatenated item features pass through a hidden layer ($2k{=}96$, ReLU) and a final projection to $\mathbb{R}^k$.
The prediction is $\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu + b_u + b_i + \mathbf{p}_u^\top \mathbf{q}_i$, where $\mathbf{p}_u$ and $\mathbf{q}_i$ are the tower outputs.

Training uses Huber loss ($\delta{=}1.0$) with Adam (initial LR $8{\times}10^{-4}$, gradient clipping at norm 1), ReduceLROnPlateau (factor 0.5, patience 3), 25\% dropout, $L_2$ regularization $10^{-5}$, rating-weighted sampling ($w \propto 1 + r_\text{scaled}^{1.25}$), 48 CNN filters per kernel, genre embedding dimension 12, and batch size 256.
The best checkpoint on validation Huber loss is restored after 40 epochs of training.

\subsection{SVD Hybrid with Ridge/Lasso}

This approach first constructs a centered interaction matrix and computes its truncated SVD to obtain initial user/item factors.
Biases are then estimated via regularized averaging of residuals.
Finally, a linear calibration layer is trained on top of the latent features:
\begin{equation}
\hat{r}_{ui} = \mathbf{w}^\top\, \phi(u,i) + w_0,
\end{equation}
where $\phi(u,i) = [\mathbf{p}_u \odot \mathbf{q}_i;\; b_u;\; b_i] \in \mathbb{R}^{k+2}$ concatenates the element-wise product of latent factors with bias terms.
The weight vector $\mathbf{w}$ is estimated by either Ridge regression (closed-form) or Lasso regression (coordinate descent).

We experiment with two configurations: \textbf{SVD-Ridge} ($k{=}32$, $\alpha{=}1.0$) and \textbf{SVD-Lasso} ($k{=}32$, $\alpha{=}0.001$, max 2{,}000 iterations).
Both use bias regularization $\lambda_b{=}25$.
The higher Ridge regularization than the typical default pushes the calibration layer toward more conservative, better-generalized predictions; we found that this substantially improves top-$N$ ranking while leaving rating accuracy largely unchanged.

\subsection{Alternating Least Squares}

ALS alternates between fixing item factors and solving for user factors, and vice versa.
Each sub-problem is a regularized least-squares system.
We augment each factor matrix with a bias column, yielding the update:
\begin{equation}
[\mathbf{p}_u; b_u] = \bigl(\mathbf{Q}_u^\top \mathbf{Q}_u + \Lambda\bigr)^{-1}\mathbf{Q}_u^\top \mathbf{y}_u,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{Q}_u$ is the matrix of item factors for items rated by user $u$, $\mathbf{y}_u$ is the vector of centered ratings, and $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda,\ldots,\lambda,\lambda_b)$ applies separate regularization to factors ($\lambda{=}12.0$) and bias ($\lambda_b{=}40.0$).
Symmetrically for item factors.
We use $k{=}4$ latent factors with a 10\% validation split and early stopping (patience 10, up to 80 iterations).
The deliberately small latent dimension and heavy regularization address ALS's tendency to overfit on small explicit-feedback datasets by constraining each closed-form solve, effectively trading memorization for generalization.

Content features account for the largest marginal contribution ($\Delta R^2 = 0.053$), driven primarily by genre indicators: Drama ($+0.092$), Animation ($+0.097$), and Documentary ($+0.066$) positively correlate with ratings, while Children ($-0.094$) and Comedy ($-0.050$) correlate negatively.

These findings motivate the Deep Hybrid model's incorporation of genre and title features in its item tower, though the relatively small $R^2$ of the full linear model (0.112) confirms that most rating variance is user- and item-specific and is better captured by latent factors.


\subsection{Latent Factor Interpretation}

We interpret the latent factors learned by MF-SGD by applying PCA to the item factor matrix $\mathbf{Q}\in\mathbb{R}^{8986\times 48}$ and correlating the top principal components with genre indicators and release year.

The first component (3.4\% of variance) separates mainstream action/comedy films (positive loadings: \textit{Miss Congeniality}, \textit{Mission: Impossible II}) from acclaimed dramas (\textit{Pulp Fiction}, \textit{Fight Club}, \textit{The Godfather}).
The second component (3.0\%) distinguishes classic musicals/dramas (\textit{Wizard of Oz}, \textit{Schindler's List}) from modern action/sci-fi blockbusters (\textit{Matrix Reloaded}).
The third component (2.9\%) captures an adventure/comedy vs.\ thriller/horror contrast (\textit{Back to the Future}, \textit{E.T.}\ vs.\ \textit{Sideways}, \textit{Panic Room}).

These semantically coherent axes emerge purely from collaborative signal, without any content features, confirming that the latent space captures meaningful preference dimensions.


\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Rating Accuracy vs.\ Ranking Quality.}
A central finding is the persistent disconnect between rating prediction accuracy and top-$N$ ranking quality, even after extensive tuning.
The Deep Hybrid model achieves the best MAE but the worst NDCG; conversely, ALS achieves the best NDCG while having competitive but not the best MAE.
This confirms that MAE/RMSE penalize errors uniformly across the rating scale, while ranking metrics care only about the relative ordering of items near the top of the list \cite{rendle2009bpr}.
Heavy regularization appears to benefit ranking by producing smoother, more conservative score distributions that better separate relevant from irrelevant items.

\paragraph{The Regularization--Ranking Connection.}
A surprising and consistent pattern emerges across all models: \textit{stronger regularization improves ranking metrics more than it hurts rating accuracy}.
Increasing Ridge $\alpha$ from 0.1 to 1.0 improved SVD-Ridge's NDCG by 37\% with less than 1\% MAE increase.
Reducing MF-SGD's $\lambda$ from 0.05 to 0.04 improved NDCG by 49\%.
Most dramatically, increasing ALS's $\lambda$ from 0.05 to 12.0 (with $k$ reduced from 48 to 4) improved NDCG by $4.4{\times}$ \textit{while simultaneously improving MAE by 27\%}.
This suggests that standard MF hyperparameter defaults are poorly calibrated for ranking on small datasets.

\paragraph{Depth vs.\ Data Scale.}
The Deep Hybrid model's weak ranking performance relative to simpler methods underscores a practical lesson: on datasets with only $\sim$100K interactions, the additional parameters of deep architectures (CNN encoders, multiple embedding layers) provide marginal benefit for ranking, even with aggressive dropout and $L_2$ regularization.
The content features (title, genre) provide limited marginal information beyond what collaborative filtering already captures, as evidenced by the ablation study's modest $R^2$.
However, the Deep model does achieve the best \emph{rating accuracy}, suggesting that its rich feature representations better capture absolute preference magnitudes even when they fail to improve relative item ordering.

\paragraph{ALS Rehabilitation.}
Our most practically significant result is the transformation of ALS from the worst to the best ranking model.
The conventional view that ALS overfits on small explicit-feedback datasets \cite{hu2008collaborative} holds only when default hyperparameters are used.
With $k{=}4$ factors (vs.\ the typical 48) and $\lambda{=}12.0$ (vs.\ the typical 0.05), validation-based early stopping, and 80 iterations, ALS achieves the highest NDCG@10 (0.157) among all models.
This finding is actionable: practitioners should consider very low-rank, heavily regularized ALS even for small-scale explicit-feedback problems.

\paragraph{Demo Application.}
We develop \textit{StreamX}, a full-stack web application consisting of a Django REST API backend and a Next.js frontend.
The backend loads all trained models and supports switching between them at runtime, serving real-time rating predictions and top-$N$ recommendations.
The frontend provides an interactive movie browsing experience with personalized recommendations, movie details enriched with TMDB metadata, and similar-movie suggestions.



\section{Limitations}

The MovieLens-Small dataset has only 610 users, which limits the statistical power of per-user ranking metrics and may not reflect behavior on larger-scale platforms.
While we conducted extensive manual hyperparameter tuning, we did not employ systematic grid or Bayesian search; even better configurations may exist.
The top-$N$ evaluation uses the \texttt{all\_test} relevance definition (all held-out items are relevant), which inflates recall denominators for users with many test items and yields low absolute metric values.
We do not explore temporal dynamics, implicit feedback signals, or side information beyond titles and genres.
The ALS finding (very few factors + heavy regularization) may not generalize to larger datasets where higher-dimensional latent spaces become necessary.


\section{Conclusion}

We presented a controlled comparison of four collaborative filtering approaches for movie recommendation on the MovieLens-Small dataset, with extensive hyperparameter tuning.
Our experiments reveal that (1) after tuning, all four model families achieve comparable rating accuracy (MAE in 0.666--0.715), with the Deep Hybrid slightly ahead; (2) the heavily regularized ALS ($k{=}4$, $\lambda{=}12.0$) achieves the best ranking performance (NDCG@10\,=\,0.157), overturning the common belief that ALS overfits on small explicit-feedback datasets; (3) SVD-Ridge with increased regularization ($\alpha{=}1.0$) also delivers strong ranking (NDCG@10\,=\,0.148); and (4) there is a consistent pattern where stronger regularization improves ranking metrics disproportionately more than it hurts rating accuracy.

The most practically significant finding is that ALS's overfitting problem is entirely solvable through aggressive regularization and dimensionality reduction: reducing factors from 48 to 4 and increasing $\lambda$ from 0.05 to 12.0 transforms ALS from the worst to the best-ranking model, while simultaneously improving its MAE by 27\%.
This suggests that standard MF hyperparameter defaults are miscalibrated for small-dataset ranking, and that practitioners should explore the low-rank, high-regularization regime.

Future work could extend this study to larger datasets to test whether the ALS regularization finding generalizes, incorporate temporal and textual review features, and explore learning-to-rank objectives that directly optimize NDCG.
